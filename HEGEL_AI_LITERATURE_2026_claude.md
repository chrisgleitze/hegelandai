# Hegel and AI: Core Literature Suggestions

_Research-state shortlist, prepared 2026-05-22. Scope: five book-length foundations and five paper/article recommendations for a Hegel-and-AI project. The selection combines Hegel's primary concepts (dialectic, recognition, objective spirit, ethical life, institutions) with current Hegelian and adjacent AI debates on LLMs, machine intelligence, subjectivity, responsibility, and human-AI interaction._

_Local project context: `/home/chris/projects/diss/` — dissertation project on institutional responsibility for AI systems. This Hegel version foregrounds recognition, Sittlichkeit, mediation, alienation, objective spirit, and the social/institutional conditions under which AI systems become normatively significant._

_Sorting: first **books**, then **articles/papers**. Format per entry: bibliographic reference, one source/full-text link, brief project-oriented summary._

---

## A. Books (AI-related Monographs and Hegelian Core Texts)

### 1. Ma, Michelle (2026): _After Recognition: Hegel, Capital and Artificial Intelligence_. Independently published.

**Type:** Short monograph / direct Hegel-and-AI title
**Source:** [Hatchards / ISBN 9798248806331](https://www.hatchards.co.uk/book/after-recognition/michelle-ma/9798248806331)
**Summary:** A direct, contemporary Hegel-and-AI monograph on recognition, capitalism, social mediation, and AI as simulated interlocutor. Because it is short and independently published rather than a canonical university-press study, it should be treated as an exploratory candidate, but it answers the narrow question whether there are book-length works explicitly titled around Hegel and artificial intelligence.

### 2. Žižek, Slavoj (2020; paperback 2021): _Hegel in A Wired Brain_. London/New York: Bloomsbury Academic.

**Type:** Monograph / Hegel and digital subjectivity
**Source:** [Bloomsbury Academic](https://www.bloomsbury.com/uk/hegel-in-a-wired-brain-9781350264045/)
**Summary:** Žižek connects Hegel to the posthuman and neuro-digital question of what happens when mental processes are externally linked to machines. The book is not only about current LLMs, but it is one of the strongest academic monographs for Hegelian thinking about digital mediation, machine-readable thought, freedom, and the status of subjectivity under technological externalization.

### 3. Negarestani, Reza (2018): _Intelligence and Spirit_. New York/Falmouth: Sequence Press / Urbanomic.

**Type:** Monograph / AGI, German Idealism, Hegelian spirit
**Source:** [Sequence Press / Urbanomic](https://www.sequencepress.com/products/intelligence-and-spirit)
**Summary:** Negarestani develops a systematic philosophy of intelligence by combining artificial general intelligence, Kant, Hegel, Sellars, and Brandom. For a Hegel-and-AI project it is indispensable because it treats intelligence and Geist as socially and functionally articulated rather than as a private mental substance. It is probably the most ambitious book-length bridge between German Idealism and AGI.

### 4. Malabou, Catherine (2019): _Morphing Intelligence: From IQ Measurement to Artificial Brains_. New York: Columbia University Press.

**Type:** Monograph / artificial brains and plasticity
**Source:** [Columbia University Press](https://cup.columbia.edu/book/morphing-intelligence/9780231187367/)
**Summary:** Malabou examines transformations of the concept of intelligence from psychometrics to neuroscience and artificial brains. The book is not titled as a Hegel monograph, but Malabou's work is deeply shaped by Hegelian dialectics and plasticity; for AI it is useful because it connects the natural/artificial boundary, brain plasticity, and the cybernetic imagination of intelligence.

### 5. Hegel, G. W. F. (1807; trans. A. V. Miller 1977): _Phenomenology of Spirit_. Oxford: Oxford University Press.

**Type:** Primary text / Hegelian core text
**Full text:** [Marxists Internet Archive edition](https://www.marxists.org/reference/archive/hegel/works/ph/phconten.htm)
**Summary:** The central source for self-consciousness, recognition, lordship and bondage, alienation, recollection, and Spirit. For Hegel and AI, it is crucial because machine intelligence becomes philosophically interesting not only as computation but as something embedded in practices of recognition, dependence, labor, language, and social mediation.

## B. Articles and Papers

### 6. Woods, Dwayne (2026): “Prompt, Negate, Repeat: A Hegelian Meditation on AI”, in: _AI & Society_ 41.4, pp. 3143–3157.

**Type:** Journal article
**Full text:** [Springer Open Access](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00146-025-02802-z)
**Summary:** Woods gives one of the most direct recent Hegelian interpretations of AI generation. The article argues that LLMs can display a structural analogy to dialectical determination, while remaining dependent on externally supplied purposes, prompts, data, and feedback. It is the best single starting point for a Hegel-and-AI one-pager after ChatGPT.

### 7. Plevrakis, Ermylos (2024): “Can AI be a subject like us? A Hegelian speculative-philosophical approach”, in: _Discover Computing_ 27, Article 46.

**Type:** Journal article
**Full text:** [Springer Open Access](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10791-024-09472-3)
**Summary:** Plevrakis uses Hegel's Philosophy of Mind to distinguish consciousness, intelligence, will, and free mind. The paper argues that Hegel offers a non-reductive framework for asking whether AI could ever become subject-like, while also stressing that current systems should not simply be equated with human spirit or free agency.

### 8. Gangle, Rocco (2022): “Backpropagation of Spirit: Hegelian Recollection and Human-A.I. Abductive Communities”, in: _Philosophies_ 7.2, Article 36.

**Type:** Journal article
**Full text:** [MDPI Open Access](https://www.mdpi.com/2409-9287/7/2/36)
**Summary:** Gangle compares Hegelian recollection, Brandomian rational reconstruction, abduction, and machine-learning backpropagation. The paper is important because it frames AI not as an isolated mind but as part of possible human-AI communities in which error correction, interpretation, and retrospective sense-making are socially organized.

### 9. Abdali, Sara / Goksen, Can / Solodko, Michael / Amizadeh, Saeed / Koishida, K. (2025): “Self-reflecting Large Language Models: A Hegelian Dialectical Approach”. arXiv:2501.14917.

**Type:** Technical paper / preprint
**Full text:** [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2501.14917)
**Summary:** This technical paper adapts a Hegelian-dialectical vocabulary to LLM self-reflection: models generate opposing viewpoints, critique them, and synthesize revised outputs. It is not Hegel scholarship in the strict sense, but it is highly useful for seeing how “Hegelian” ideas are entering prompt engineering, multi-agent deliberation, and automated critique pipelines.

### 10. Winfield, Richard Dien (2009): “Hegel, Mind, and Mechanism: Why Machines Have No Psyche, Consciousness, or Intelligence”, in: _Hegel Bulletin_ 30.1–2, pp. 1–18.

**Type:** Journal article
**Source:** [Cambridge Core](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/hegel-bulletin/article/hegel-mind-and-mechanism-why-machines-have-no-psyche-consciousness-or-intelligence/4656840E8FA62CACB0AC275219819A43)
**Summary:** Winfield is an important counterweight to more expansive Hegelian AI readings. From Hegel's philosophy of mind, he argues against reducing psyche, consciousness, and intelligence to mechanism. For the website, this entry marks the negative pole: AI may participate in human social practices, but that does not by itself make machines bearers of mind, freedom, or responsibility.
