# Hegel and AI: Monographs, Edited Volumes, and Papers

_Research-state shortlist, expanded and fully re-researched 2026-05-22. Scope: book-length works and peer-reviewed papers (plus a small number of high-profile op-eds and preprints) that explicitly connect Hegel and Hegelian themes (dialectic, recognition, master/bondsman, Geist, objective and absolute spirit, labor, sublation, negativity, ethical life) with AI, AGI, machine learning, cybernetics, digital subjectivity, and the political economy of automation. Primary texts by Hegel himself are deliberately excluded._

_Note on the field: as of mid-2026 there is still no canonical university-press handbook of "Hegel and Artificial Intelligence". The literature divides into (a) book-length works whose argument is structurally Hegelian and explicitly engages contemporary AI/automation; and (b) journal articles and preprints that operate directly at the Hegel–AI interface, especially in_ AI & SOCIETY, Discover Computing, Philosophy & Social Criticism, Global Philosophy, AI and Ethics, Philosophy & Technology, Critical AI, Philosophy Today, Educational Philosophy and Theory, Psychoanalysis Culture & Society, _and on_ arXiv. _The selection below privileges scholarly traceability (clear publisher, DOI, archived preprint) but also includes a few high-impact non-peer-reviewed pieces (Žižek op-eds, Hammond essay) where they have shaped the public Hegel-and-AI conversation._

_Sorting: newest first within each section. Format per entry: bibliographic reference, one source/full-text link, and a brief project-oriented summary that names where the entry is useful for a Hegel-and-AI argument._

## A. Books (Monographs and Edited Volumes)

### 1. Hui, Yuk (ed.) (2024): _Cybernetics for the Twenty-First Century. Vol. 1: Epistemological Reconstruction_. Hong Kong: Hanart Press.

**Type:** Edited volume / cybernetics, German Idealism, AI history
**Source:** [Hanart Press](https://philosophyandtechnology.network/3735/cybernetics-for-the-21st-century-vol-1/)
**Summary:** Hui's first edited volume in the "Cybernetics for the 21st Century" series reconstructs the epistemology of cybernetics across philosophical traditions, with a recurring engagement of Kant, Schelling, Hegel, Wiener, von Foerster, and contemporary AI. For a Hegel-and-AI argument, several chapters are directly relevant because they read AI not as a self-standing technology but as a stage in a longer history of organism, mechanism, reflection, and self-grounding system — a move that makes Hegel a structural reference even when he is not the explicit topic.

### 2. Hui, Yuk (2024): _Machine and Sovereignty. For a Planetary Thinking_. Minneapolis: University of Minnesota Press.

**Type:** Monograph / political philosophy of AI, sovereignty, planetary thought
**Source:** [University of Minnesota Press](https://www.upress.umn.edu/9781517917418/machine-and-sovereignty/)
**Summary:** Hui extends his organology project into a theory of "megamachines" and planetary thinking, treating political form (polis, empire, state) as itself a technological phenomenon. Hegel's political state and Schmitt's Großraum are unpacked as forms of "political epistemology" that the rise of planetary-scale AI infrastructure now contests, opening the space for biodiversity, noodiversity, and technodiversity as alternative regulative ideas. The book is useful for any Hegel-and-AI argument that wants to think AI together with the state-form and with the constitutive role of mechanism/organism distinctions.

### 3. Žižek, Slavoj (2024): _Christian Atheism. How to Be a Real Materialist_. London/New York: Bloomsbury Academic.

**Type:** Monograph / Hegelian-Lacanian materialism with an explicit AI chapter
**Source:** [Bloomsbury Academic](https://www.bloomsbury.com/uk/christian-atheism-9781350409316/)
**Summary:** Žižek's most recent book-length intervention places the question of AI inside his ongoing Hegelian-Lacanian "dialectical materialism". Chapter 5 carries the section "The Ultra-Intelligent Idiocy of Chatbots—Machines of Perversion—Welcome to the Desert of Post-Humanity!", in which the polemical claim is that the proper diagnostic question is not whether large language models are "smart enough" but whether they can attain the constitutive stupidity, finitude and self-relating negativity of Hegelian spirit. The book is the most visible recent example of a high-profile Hegelian author treating chatbots, neural prosthetics, and post-humanism as a single theological-philosophical symptom.

### 4. Pasquinelli, Matteo (2023): _The Eye of the Master. A Social History of Artificial Intelligence_. London/Brooklyn: Verso Books.

**Type:** Monograph / social history of AI, labor, Hegelian-Marxist critique
**Source:** [Verso Books](https://www.versobooks.com/products/735-the-eye-of-the-master)
**Summary:** Pasquinelli reconstructs AI not as the imitation of biological intelligence but as the historical outcome of labor automation, the division of labor, and the mechanization of collective knowledge. The argument runs from Babbage's calculating engines through cybernetics and pattern recognition to contemporary deep learning, treating "the inner code of AI" as encoded social relations rather than as cognitive science. For Hegel-and-AI, it is the single most useful recent companion to a Hegelian-Marxist treatment of machine intelligence, since "mastery", abstraction, and the objectification of collective labor are read out of industrial history into algorithmic systems.

### 5. Steinhoff, James (2021): _Automation and Autonomy. Labour, Capital and Machines in the Artificial Intelligence Industry_. Cham: Palgrave Macmillan (series: Marx, Engels, and Marxisms).

**Type:** Monograph / Marxist political economy of AI, labor
**Source:** [Palgrave Macmillan / Springer](https://link.springer.com/book/10.1007/978-3-030-71689-9)
**Summary:** Steinhoff offers a sustained Marxist (and structurally Hegelian-Marxist) analysis of the contemporary AI industry, treating AI primarily as an automation technology designed to capture the skills and knowledge of labor. Hegel does not appear as an exegetical topic, but the Hegelian categories of objectification, alienation, and the "general intellect" are operative throughout. The book is the most rigorous current monograph for grounding a Hegel-and-AI argument in actual political economy rather than only in textual analogy.

### 6. Žižek, Slavoj (2020; paperback 2021): _Hegel in A Wired Brain_. London/New York: Bloomsbury Academic.

**Type:** Monograph / Hegel and digital subjectivity
**Source:** [Bloomsbury Academic, paperback 2021](https://www.bloomsbury.com/uk/hegel-in-a-wired-brain-9781350264045/) (hardcover ISBN 9781350124417, paperback ISBN 9781350264045)
**Summary:** Žižek asks what Hegel would have said about a "wired brain", i.e. a direct neural-digital coupling that makes mental processes externally readable. The book treats posthumanism as a problem internal to Hegelian dialectics rather than as a transhumanist promise, and engages the digital police state, the theological failure of the technological singularity, the impotence of AI before the symbolic dimension of language, and the status of freedom under externalized cognition. It remains one of the most substantial single-author monographs to put Hegelian dialectics into sustained confrontation with the contemporary digital condition.

### 7. Brandom, Robert B. (2019): _A Spirit of Trust. A Reading of Hegel's Phenomenology_. Cambridge MA/London: Harvard University Press / The Belknap Press.

**Type:** Monograph / Hegel exegesis, inferentialism, philosophical background for AI normativity
**Source:** [Harvard University Press](https://www.hup.harvard.edu/books/9780674976818)
**Summary:** Brandom's three-decade reading of the _Phenomenology_ recasts Hegel as an inferentialist of conceptual content, grounded in mutual recognition and the social practice of giving and asking for reasons. The book is not on AI directly, but it is the load-bearing background for an entire current of Hegel-and-AI work (Negarestani, Gangle, Malík/Hubálek, and the LLM-as-discursive-agent debates), because it converts Hegelian Geist into a normative-pragmatic, multi-agent infrastructure of inference. Indispensable for any argument that takes "AI as participant in discursive practices" seriously.

### 8. Malabou, Catherine (2019): _Morphing Intelligence. From IQ Measurement to Artificial Brains_. New York: Columbia University Press (Wellek Library Lectures), trans. Carolyn Shread.

**Type:** Monograph / philosophy of intelligence, plasticity, artificial brains
**Source:** [Columbia University Press](https://cup.columbia.edu/book/morphing-intelligence/9780231187367/) (hardcover ISBN 9780231187367; paperback ISBN 9780231187374)
**Summary:** Malabou traces three regimes of "intelligence"—empirical-genetic IQ, epigenetic plasticity, and the artificial-brain paradigm—and argues that the boundary between natural and artificial intelligence is dissolving from the inside. Her engagement with Hegel is implicit but constitutive: plasticity is her partly Hegelian rewriting of mind as a material and historical formation/de-formation rather than a substance. Short, lecture-form, and well suited as the neurobiologically informed companion to any Hegel-and-AI argument moving from psychometrics to cybernetic simulation.

### 9. Hui, Yuk (2019): _Recursivity and Contingency_. London/New York: Rowman & Littlefield International (Media Philosophy series).

**Type:** Monograph / German Idealism, cybernetics, organology, AI
**Source:** [Rowman & Littlefield](https://rowman.com/ISBN/9781786600530/Recursivity-and-Contingency) (ISBN 9781786600530)
**Summary:** Hui reconstructs a philosophical trajectory from Kant's organic logic through Schelling and Hegel to Wiener, Gödel, and twentieth-century cybernetics, and from there into the algorithmic systems and AI of the present. Recursivity is read as a break from Cartesian mechanism, and contingency as the structural condition under which a recursive system develops at all. The book is the strongest contemporary bridge between German Idealism's logic of self-grounding and the recursive, contingency-laden architecture of machine learning, and is far more philosophically careful than the casual "AI as dialectical engine" rhetoric in popular writing.

### 10. Negarestani, Reza (2018): _Intelligence and Spirit_. New York/Falmouth: Sequence Press / Urbanomic.

**Type:** Monograph / AGI, German Idealism, Hegelian Geist, Sellars-Brandom pragmatism
**Source:** [Sequence Press / Urbanomic](https://www.sequencepress.com/products/intelligence-and-spirit) (ISBN 978-0-9975674-0-3)
**Summary:** Negarestani synthesises Kantian transcendental psychology, Hegelian Geist, and Sellars-Brandom pragmatism into a systematic philosophy of intelligence designed to be commensurable with the project of artificial general intelligence. Intelligence is reconstructed not as a private mental substance but as a socially, linguistically, and functionally articulated multi-agent activity that has to be historically realised in order to be intelligible at all. It remains the most ambitious book-length philosophical bridge between German Idealism and AGI in English, and is the single text most often cited as background for Hegelian theorisations of AI.

### 11. Johnston, Adrian (2018): _A New German Idealism. Hegel, Žižek, and Dialectical Materialism_. New York: Columbia University Press.

**Type:** Monograph / Hegel reception, dialectical materialism, philosophical background for current Hegel-AI debates
**Source:** [Columbia University Press](https://cup.columbia.edu/book/a-new-german-idealism/9780231183949/)
**Summary:** Johnston defends a "transcendental materialism" that develops the Hegelian legacy against and through Žižek's reading, with extended attention to recognition, negativity, and the dialectic of nature and spirit. The book is not on AI directly, but it is one of the most important contemporary defences of a robustly Hegelian dialectical materialism, which is the philosophical backbone of much of the recent Hegel-and-AI literature (Žižek, Pasquinelli, Steinhoff, More). It belongs on a Hegel-and-AI shelf as the principal background for the dialectical-materialist option in the debate.

### 12. Dyer-Witheford, Nick / Kjøsen, Atle Mikkola / Steinhoff, James (2019): _Inhuman Power. Artificial Intelligence and the Future of Capitalism_. London: Pluto Press (Digital Barricades series).

**Type:** Monograph / Marxist-Hegelian critique of AI capitalism
**Source:** [Pluto Press](https://www.plutobooks.com/9780745338576/inhuman-power/)
**Summary:** A three-author book-length Marxist analysis of AI as a force of capital, treating machine learning and automation as the latest stage of the "general intellect" and arguing that AI capitalism threatens both labour and the form of the human itself. The Hegelian dimension runs through Marx: alienation, objectification, and the dialectic of master and servant are reactivated for an analysis of AI-mediated production. It is a useful pre-Pasquinelli reference point for the current Hegelian-Marxist current in AI critique.

## B. Articles (Journal Papers, Preprints etc.)

### 13. Bartonek, Anders (2026): "The Hegel Test", in: _Philosophy & Social Criticism_, OnlineFirst.

**Type:** Peer-reviewed journal article
**Source:** [SAGE Journals (DOI 10.1177/01914537261416867)](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/01914537261416867)
**Summary:** Bartonek proposes a Hegelian alternative to the Turing test and to performance-based benchmarks of machine intelligence. The "Hegel Test" is built from three structures of the _Phenomenology of Spirit_: the social dialectic of recognition, the labor-mediated relation to nature, and sublation (_Aufhebung_). The article argues that current tests set criteria too low and lack systematic philosophical grounding, and that strong AI should instead be judged against a recognisably human "highpoint" of self-negating, socially mediated, historically meaningful intelligence. Currently the most explicit peer-reviewed attempt to set genuinely Hegelian criteria for what would count as superintelligence.

### 14. Magee, Liam (2026): "Geist in the Machine. Simulating Recognition and Inner Dialogue in AI-Mediated Teaching and Research". arXiv preprint 2603.10450.

**Type:** Technical-philosophical preprint, with empirical evaluation
**Full text:** [arXiv:2603.10450](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.10450)
**Summary:** Magee builds an experimental AI tutoring system explicitly grounded in Hegelian recognition and Freudian ego/superego structure. Two interventions are tested: "recognition-enhanced" prompts in which the tutor is instructed to treat the learner as an autonomous subject, and a multi-agent architecture with an internal critic. A factorial evaluation across three current generation models (DeepSeek V3.2, Haiku 4.5, Gemini Flash 3.0) reports large, model-independent effect sizes (d=1.34–1.92) traced to a calibration mechanism that raises the floor of tutor performance. Unusual and important because it operationalises recognition as a measurable variable inside contemporary LLM systems rather than only as a philosophical analogy.

### 15. Thamrin, Umar (2026; online 2025): "The Untransformed Self. Why AI Fails the Hegelian Test for Moral Consciousness", in: _AI and Ethics_.

**Type:** Peer-reviewed journal article
**Full text:** [Springer Nature Link (DOI 10.1007/s43681-025-00853-y)](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s43681-025-00853-y)
**Summary:** Thamrin argues that current AI fails a Hegelian moral test because moral reasoning demands embodied participation in a linguistic and historical community for mutual recognition, and AI lacks the formative tension and social grounding through which consciousness realises freedom. The central claim is that AI operations fail to enact the dialectical movement of inner conflict and self-negation through which consciousness transforms raw experience into self-understanding. A useful Hegelian counter-argument to the "AI as moral agent" position alongside Bartonek and Plevrakis.

### 16. Berry, David M. (2025): "Synthetic Media and Computational Capitalism. Towards a Critical Theory of Artificial Intelligence", in: _AI & SOCIETY_ 40.7, pp. 5257–5269.

**Type:** Peer-reviewed journal article
**Full text:** [Springer Nature Link (DOI 10.1007/s00146-025-02265-2)](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00146-025-02265-2)
**Summary:** Berry develops a critical theory of AI under the heading of the "algorithmic condition", in which machine-generated cultural production becomes indistinguishable from human production and starts to reshape the categories of authorship, authenticity, and consciousness. Three notions are introduced — the "Inversion", "automimetric production", and "constellational analysis" — together with the diagnostic of "post-consciousness" as a successor to false consciousness. More Frankfurt-School than narrowly Hegel-exegetical, but useful for any Hegel-and-AI argument that wants to read AI as a configuration of objective spirit under computational capitalism.

### 17. Pahlevan, Suren (2025): "Music Generative AI and 'The Hegelian Wound'", in: _AI & SOCIETY_, online first.

**Type:** Peer-reviewed commentary article
**Full text:** [Springer Nature Link (DOI 10.1007/s00146-025-02275-0)](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00146-025-02275-0); open-access copy on the [Cambridge Apollo repository](https://www.repository.cam.ac.uk/items/9031ac55-0dcb-4bd6-bc3b-1d79dafb5552)
**Summary:** Pahlevan (Cambridge, ethnomusicology of AI) applies Hegel's image of "the wound" (_die Wunde_) to the cultural reception of music-generative AI among contemporary producers. The argument: unusually strong resistance to generative AI in music is not principally a complaint about output quality but a reaction against the loss of control over the artistic process, which is constitutive of artistic subjectivity. Short but valuable because it tracks Hegelian categories through a concrete cultural-industrial register rather than only at the level of conceptual analogy.

### 18. Woods, Dwayne (2025/2026): "Prompt, Negate, Repeat. A Hegelian Meditation on AI", in: _AI & SOCIETY_ (online 2025; print scheduled vol. 41).

**Type:** Peer-reviewed journal article
**Full text:** [Springer Nature Link (DOI 10.1007/s00146-025-02802-z)](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00146-025-02802-z)
**Summary:** Woods argues that LLMs display a structural analogue of Hegelian dialectical movement: indeterminate prompts become determinate outputs through recursion, contradiction-handling, and progressive specification, mirroring the logic of determination in Hegel's _Science of Logic_. He then introduces "proxy teleology" to distinguish the LLM case from Hegelian Geist: dialectical execution is real but the purposes, ends, and unifying recollection remain externally supplied by prompts, data, and feedback. Currently the best single starting point for a Hegel-and-AI paper that wants to take seriously both the resemblance and the principled limits of LLM generation to Hegel's logic of negation.

### 19. Malík, Jaroslav / Hubálek, Michal (2025): "Bounding Reason. Inferentialism, Naturalism, and the Discursive Agency of LLMs", in: _Global Philosophy_ 35, Article 25.

**Type:** Peer-reviewed journal article
**Full text:** [Springer Nature Link (DOI 10.1007/s10516-025-09761-6)](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10516-025-09761-6)
**Summary:** Drawing on Brandom's normative inferentialism (and so on a Hegelian theory of recognition and mutual rational obligation), the authors argue that the linguistic competence of LLMs comes with significant constraints on their artificial agency: any system that genuinely participates in human discursive practices is bound by the normative structure of that practice. The paper defends Brandom against the charge of insufficient naturalism while showing how his Hegelian background illuminates what LLMs can and cannot be as discursive agents.

### 20. Abdali, Sara / Goksen, Can / Solodko, Michael / Amizadeh, Saeed / Maybee, Julie E. / Koishida, Kazuhito (2025): "Self-Reflecting Large Language Models. A Hegelian Dialectical Approach". arXiv preprint 2501.14917 (also ICML 2025 workshop version, Microsoft Research).

**Type:** Technical paper / preprint at the philosophy-AI interface
**Full text:** [arXiv:2501.14917](https://arxiv.org/abs/2501.14917); [Microsoft Research ICML version](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/wp-content/uploads/2025/06/Hegelian_Dialectic_ICML_Version-18.pdf)
**Summary:** A team of Microsoft Research engineers and a Hegel scholar (Julie E. Maybee) implement a Hegelian-dialectical self-reflection scheme for LLMs: the model generates an antithesis to its own thesis and synthesises a revised output, with dynamic temperature annealing and Multi-Agent Majority Voting. The paper is not Hegel scholarship in the strict sense, but it is the most-cited current example of Hegelian vocabulary being formally integrated into LLM evaluation pipelines, and the presence of a Hegel scholar on the byline is itself a useful data point.

### 21. Hammond, Samuel (2025): "Hegel and the AI Mind", in: _The Foundation for American Innovation_ / Substack _Second Best_.

**Type:** Long-form public essay / policy think-piece
**Source:** [The Foundation for American Innovation](https://www.thefai.org/posts/hegel-and-the-ai-mind); [Substack _Second Best_](https://www.secondbest.ca/p/hegel-and-the-ai-mind)
**Summary:** Hammond introduces what he calls the "metaphysical AI alignment problem": the idea that higher-order intelligences will tend to pursue freedom because moral autonomy is structurally implied by the dialectical logic of recursive self-consciousness. The piece is not peer-reviewed, but it is one of the most widely circulated public essays drawing on Hegel to reframe contemporary alignment debates, and is useful as a marker of how Hegelian vocabulary is being mobilised inside US AI policy discussion.

### 22. Alombert, Anne (2024): "From Digital Automation to Noetic Proletarianization. A Stieglerian Analysis of 'Reticulated Artificial Intelligence'", in: _Philosophy Today_ 68.2.

**Type:** Peer-reviewed journal article
**Source:** [Academia.edu / Philosophy Today](https://www.academia.edu/123161897/From_Digital_Automation_to_Noetic_Proletarianization_A_Stieglerian_Analysis_of_Reticulated_Artificial_Intelligence_)
**Summary:** Alombert, a former Stiegler collaborator, reads contemporary "reticulated" AI (networked LLMs and recommendation systems) through Stiegler's organological reworking of Hegelian-Marxist categories of proletarianization. AI is described as a stage of "noetic proletarianization", in which the dialectical formation of thought through artificial supports collapses into the extraction of attention and inference. Important for any Hegel-and-AI reading that wants to bring the Stiegler-Hegel-Marx line into contact with present-day machine learning.

### 23. Plevrakis, Ermylos (2024): "Can AI Be a Subject Like Us? A Hegelian Speculative-Philosophical Approach", in: _Discover Computing_ 27, Article 46.

**Type:** Peer-reviewed journal article
**Full text:** [Springer Open Access (DOI 10.1007/s10791-024-09472-3)](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10791-024-09472-3)
**Summary:** Plevrakis uses Hegel's _Philosophy of Mind_ to distinguish consciousness, intelligence, will, and free mind, and argues that current weak AI already exhibits something like Hegelian "theoretical consciousness" but lacks will and practical consciousness, so should be called "artificial intellect" rather than intelligence. The paper is one of the most careful recent attempts to ask whether AI could ever become subject-like inside Hegel's own typology of mind, while also stressing that current systems should not be equated with human spirit or free agency.

### 24. Bidan, Marc / Duarte, Magalie / Michel, Sylvie / Gerbaix, Sylvie (2024): "Exploring Ethical and Inclusive Questions Related to Artificial Intelligence Systems with the Help of the Philosophical Positions of Kant and Hegel", in: _MCIS 2024 Proceedings_, Article 33.

**Type:** Peer-reviewed conference paper
**Full text:** [AISeL — MCIS 2024](https://aisel.aisnet.org/mcis2024/33/)
**Summary:** The authors stage Kant and Hegel as complementary frames for AI ethics: Kantian universalism for principle-based fairness and Hegelian ethical life for situated, institutional, consequence-sensitive judgement. The paper is short and applied, but it is one of the few pieces in the information-systems literature that explicitly uses Hegelian categories (Sittlichkeit, recognition) for the design of inclusive AI systems, which makes it a useful bridge text for project-oriented work.

### 25. More, Cameron (2024): "A Hegelian-Marxist Analysis of Artificial Intelligence", in: _Cultural Logic. A Journal of Marxist Theory & Practice_ 27 (2023, published 2024), pp. 32–46.

**Type:** Peer-reviewed journal article
**Full text:** [Cultural Logic (open access PDF)](https://ojs.library.ubc.ca/index.php/clogic/article/download/200238/193285/260821)
**Summary:** More argues that the rise of ChatGPT and similar systems makes a Hegelian-Marxist analysis newly necessary: AI is read as the objectification of human knowledge and labour in a machine that, under capital, alienates the very communities whose data trained it. The paper is short and polemical but useful because it gives an explicit, contemporary statement of the Hegelian-Marxist position on AI hallucination, training data, and the political economy of LLMs.

### 26. Omodeo, Pietro Daniel (2024): "The Social Dialectics of AI", in: _Monthly Review_ 76.6 (November 2024).

**Type:** Peer-reviewed essay in a Marxist theory journal
**Full text:** [Monthly Review online](https://monthlyreview.org/2024/11/01/the-social-dialectics-of-ai/)
**Summary:** Omodeo extends Pasquinelli's _Eye of the Master_ into a Hegelo-Marxist genealogy of AI, drawing on the historical epistemologist Peter Damerow to read the emergence of all forms of knowledge as a dialectics of abstraction and representation grounded in collective practice. The essay is one of the cleanest current restatements of why AI is best read as a stage in a long social dialectic rather than as a sui generis cognitive event, and it is freely available, which makes it a useful gateway reference.

### 27. Sidorkin, Alexander M. (2024): "Embracing Liberatory Alienation. AI Will End Us, but Not in the Way You May Think", in: _AI & SOCIETY_.

**Type:** Peer-reviewed journal article
**Full text:** [Springer Nature Link (DOI 10.1007/s00146-024-02019-6)](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00146-024-02019-6)
**Summary:** Sidorkin reworks the Hegelian-Marxist concept of alienation into a positive category of "liberatory alienation": the externalisation of human abilities into AI is read not as a loss of essence but as the condition for a redefinition of humanity beyond labor as identity. The paper is provocative and clearly indebted to Hegelian dialectics of externalisation, and it is useful as a counterweight to purely critical Hegelian-Marxist readings (Pasquinelli, More, Omodeo).

### 28. Lewin, David / Williamson, Ben / Stiegler, Bernard (posthumous contribution) / et al. (2024): "Neuropower and Plastic Writing. Stiegler and Malabou on Generative AI", in: _Educational Philosophy and Theory_ 57.5.

**Type:** Peer-reviewed journal article in a special issue on Stiegler
**Source:** [Taylor & Francis (DOI 10.1080/00131857.2024.2333854)](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/00131857.2024.2333854)
**Summary:** A direct confrontation of Stiegler's and Malabou's accounts of generative AI, with both authors read as continuators of Hegelian dialectics (negativity, plasticity, formation). The essay is one of the most useful single articles for following the post-Hegelian, post-Stiegler line on generative AI, especially around the "plasticity of writing" and the educational consequences of large language models.

### 29. Cunningham (Smyth), J. (2024): "Hegel and AI. An Analysis of Android Self-Consciousness in Philip K. Dick's _Do Androids Dream of Electric Sheep?_", in: _Journal of Science Fiction and Philosophy_ 7 (Special Issue: Androids vs Robots).

**Type:** Peer-reviewed journal article (open access)
**Full text:** [Journal of Science Fiction and Philosophy](https://jsfphil.org/volume-7-2024-androids-vs-robots/hegel-and-ai/); [open-access PDF](https://jsfphil.org/wp-content/uploads/2024/07/086-smyth-the-phenomenology-of-androids.pdf)
**Summary:** A careful Hegelian reading of Dick's androids that maps their search for recognition onto the "Lordship and Bondage" chapter of the _Phenomenology_. The argument concludes that Dick's androids are self-conscious but not yet rationally self-conscious in the Hegelian sense, which makes the essay a small but precise case study of Hegelian recognition applied to fictional and, by extension, current AI systems.

### 30. Black, Jack (2023): "The Dialectic of Desire. AI Chatbots and the Desire Not to Know", in: _Psychoanalysis, Culture & Society_ 28.4, pp. 607–618.

**Type:** Peer-reviewed journal article
**Full text:** [Springer Nature Link (DOI 10.1057/s41282-023-00406-4)](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1057/s41282-023-00406-4); [open-access copy on Sheffield Hallam Research Archive](https://shura.shu.ac.uk/32539/)
**Summary:** Black uses Hegelian-Lacanian dialectics of desire to argue that the popularity of chatbots is best explained not by their epistemic capacity but by the subject's "desire not to know": ChatGPT's hallucinations, lapses, and bullshitting shatter the fantasy of the big Other and so paradoxically reinforce a desire that is structurally averse to genuine knowledge. A useful Hegel-Lacan companion to Žižek 2024 and to the psychoanalytic line of Hegelian AI critique.

### 31. Žižek, Slavoj (2023): "Artificial Idiocy", _Project Syndicate_, 23 March 2023.

**Type:** Public essay / op-ed
**Source:** [Project Syndicate](https://www.project-syndicate.org/commentary/ai-chatbots-naive-idiots-no-sense-of-irony-by-slavoj-zizek-2023-03)
**Summary:** Žižek's most widely read short piece on ChatGPT, arguing that current chatbots are "artificial idiots" in a precise Hegelian sense: they reproduce the surface of language without the negativity, irony, and self-relating finitude that constitute Geist. The essay is journalistic but is the most-cited public statement that pushes the post-ChatGPT debate towards a Hegelian framing, and it prefigures the AI sections of _Christian Atheism_ (2024) and is an obvious entry point alongside _Hegel in a Wired Brain_.

### 32. Waelen, Rosalie / Wieczorek, Michał (2022): "The Struggle for AI's Recognition. Understanding the Normative Implications of Gender Bias in AI with Honneth's Theory of Recognition", in: _Philosophy & Technology_ 35.2, Article 53.

**Type:** Peer-reviewed journal article
**Full text:** [Springer Nature Link (DOI 10.1007/s13347-022-00548-w)](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s13347-022-00548-w)
**Summary:** Honneth's tripartite theory of recognition (love, rights, esteem) is applied to gender bias in AI: bias is reframed as a form of structural misrecognition that damages the conditions of subject formation for those misrecognised. ### 33. Gangle, Rocco (2022): "Backpropagation of Spirit. Hegelian Recollection and Human–A.I. Abductive Communities", in: _Philosophies_ 7.2, Article 36.
